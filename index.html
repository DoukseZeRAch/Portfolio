My foundation was architecture — a discipline I defined as une science sensible, un art sensé : where technical rigour and human sensitivity are not opposites, but the same thing seen from different angles.

It taught me one principle. Design the constraint system before designing the output. Understand the failure modes before building the features.

That principle has never left me.

It followed me through a decade of projection mapping and creative direction — systems where output failure is visible in real time, in front of an audience, with no rollback. You do not hope for reliability. You design it in.

When I moved into AI, the translation was direct.

Today I design the invisible architectures that make AI produce meaningful output. Prompt engineering, as I practice it, is a form of Socratic dialogue — a maieutic art guided by semantic equations that reduce the infinite probabilistic space of a language model into a precise, intentional response.

A prompt library is not a collection of instructions. It is the mechanism that determines what the model will and will not produce — at scale, without review, before the first user encounters an edge case.

I have built four systems on this principle.

A psychometric pipeline where the scoring logic is encoded into prompt constraints across 11 supported languages. A behavioural simulation where state-driven prompting maintains psychological coherence with real teenagers under real social pressure. An HR analytics platform where framework anchoring produces structured, consulting-style analysis instead of generic advice. An automated intelligence pipeline where a large profile-as-prompt systematises the decision framework behind 600+ daily decisions.

Each one co-designed — not with a brief, but with the domain expert who owned the knowledge the system needed to encode. Each one in production. Each one iterated from failure, not from assumption.

I do not optimize prompts.
I design the architectures that make output reliable by default.

Every AI system makes an implicit promise to its users: the output will be useful, consistent, and safe. Most cannot keep it.

Not because the model is wrong.
Because nobody designed the architecture that makes the promise true by default.

That gap — between a prompt that works in a demo and a system that holds under volume — is not a model problem. It is a design problem. Output that impresses at launch and degrades at scale. Edge cases ignored in development that become the dominant pattern in production. Failure modes that nobody named because nobody asked what the system must never produce.

Most teams discover the gap when users find it.
I find it before the first prompt is written.

I have built constraint architectures for a psychometric career platform serving young people across 11 languages, for an educational simulation that must maintain psychological coherence with real teenagers in real time, for an HR analytics platform where output must be calibrated to the language and standards of organisational leadership, and for an automated intelligence pipeline that handles 600+ daily decisions with minimal routine human review.

In each case the work began before the first prompt and continued until the failure modes were known, named, and addressed structurally rather than relying on manual oversight.

My intention is to do that work for your system. And to be precise enough about what can go wrong that you know exactly what you are getting before we begin.

Avance

AI Career Guidance Platform · avance.today

Career guidance for young people has long relied on generic personality tests that tell you who you are — but not what to do next. Avance was built to close that gap.

The platform guides users through a structured self-assessment across 18 open questions. Each response is scored by GPT-4o-mini against a 9-dimensional psychometric model, then mapped using vector similarity against the ESCO corpus — with a diversification step to avoid near-duplicate matches across 4,797 occupational profiles.

The prompt architecture is the core of the system. 111 prompt files across 11 languages — each one native, not translated. A prompt in French thinks in French. In our design work, prompts in Arabic ended up using somewhat different constraint patterns than prompts in Dutch, reflecting language and context considerations rather than a universal rule.

The key engineering decision was the INTERDICTIONS section — a dedicated constraint layer, separate from the instructions, that reduced output drift on psychometric scoring tasks where positive instructions alone were insufficient. An observation from production, scoped to this specific task type.

Temperature is calibrated per task type — lower for scoring and analysis, higher for narrative generation. Each prompt type carries its own persona and output schema.

The system was co-designed with a career coach specialising in youth guidance — ensuring that the AI output was not only technically sound, but genuinely useful in a real accompaniment context.

Deployed in production. Available in 11 languages.

ShieldMind

Cyberbullying Prevention Simulation · shieldmind.eu

Most anti-bullying programmes tell young people what to think. ShieldMind puts them inside the situation.

The platform simulates a real online conversation with AI characters that play out the dynamics of cyberbullying in real time. Students don't observe the scenario — they participate in it, making decisions under social pressure, in the same conditions where bullying actually happens.

The prompt architecture is built on three distinct systems. The initiation system sets the scenario. The dynamic response system handles real-time character AI. The analytical debrief system extracts teachable moments from the full session transcript.

The key architectural decision was state-driven prompting: the accumulated context of AI characters is structured and carried across the session so that each prompt call reflects the current psychological state. Characters are modelled not just with static personalities but with evolving histories.

The Karpman Drama Triangle — persecutor, victim, rescuer — operates as the primary AI behaviour engine, encoded mainly in the prompt architecture rather than in dedicated application logic. Hundreds of behavioural coefficients, sourced from academic research on cyberbullying dynamics and co-designed with a Rome-based research team, shape how characters respond, escalate, and shift roles.

In deployments across 15 schools in France and Italy, preliminary measurements suggested up to a +42% improvement in student intervention behaviour, subject to the usual limits of sample size and study design.

Hidden Team

Performance Diagnostic · B2B HR Analytics

Every team has a surface story — the one that appears in performance reviews and management reports. And then there is what actually drives it: the unspoken dynamics, the invisible tensions, the patterns that no survey has ever been designed to catch.

The Hidden Team Performance Diagnostic was built to surface the second story.

The system runs 9 specialised AI analyzers, each anchored in a recognised framework of organisational psychology: Edmondson's psychological safety model, Lencioni's trust framework, Tuckman's team development stages, and Google's Project Aristotle research. The prompt architecture encodes the main analytical logic of the methodology, with supporting computations handled outside the model when needed — the frameworks are not referenced for credibility; they are the constraint structure that shapes the output.

The key engineering decision was a server-side pre-aggregation layer: statistical processing of survey responses is handled before the LLM call. GPT receives structured data and interprets it through framework-specific constraints — rather than deriving statistics itself.

A priority distribution forcing mechanism steers each session toward producing insights across five categories — pattern, strength, challenge, recommendation, risk — reducing the tendency to collapse everything into a single type of recommendation. In practice: 46 structured insights per complete analysis session, generated in approximately 45 seconds.

The diagnostic framework was co-designed with a specialist in team performance and organisational development — whose expertise shaped both the questions asked and the analytical logic applied to the responses.

Deployed in production for a corporate client.

Job Radar

Automated Intelligence Pipeline · Prompt Engineering at System Scale

Job Radar is a personal AI-powered job monitoring system built to demonstrate what prompt engineering looks like when applied to an automated pipeline — not a product for users, but a system that makes decisions at scale on behalf of a single, highly specific profile.

The system scans multiple job boards daily, fetches raw listings via RSS and API feeds, and passes them through a multi-stage pre-filter pipeline — spam, relevance, location and salary constraints — before any LLM call is made. This reduces 780 raw items to 46 GPT calls per scan — a 94% reduction in token cost, while maintaining an internal hit rate of around 76% on relevant results over the evaluated period.

The scoring engine is built on a dedicated profile prompt that encodes the full decision framework: identity, skills, ideal roles, strong-fit keywords, contract preferences, location rules, sector exclusions, and a dimensional scoring rubric across multiple independent axes. This is the profile-as-prompt pattern: instead of asking the AI to decide, you encode your decision framework into the prompt and ask it to apply it consistently.

The drafter — a second GPT system — generates application messages structured around tactical empathy, concrete proof from a project library, and low-friction calls to action. The system includes few-shot examples and anti-patterns to guide output quality.

Job Radar is not a product. It is a working demonstration of how prompt engineering can significantly reduce the need for human judgment in a repetitive, high-volume decision process, while preserving enough precision to keep the output useful.
